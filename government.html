Our Government

The Lummi Tribe of the Lummi Reservation ratified a constitution and bylaws in 1970, which was amended in 2006. The tribe is governed by the Lummi Indian Business Council (LIBC), an elected 11-member council on which members serve three-year terms.

The Council selects executive officers for their duties, including Chair, Vice Chair, Treasurer, and Secretary, and establishes committees for administration of the tribe.

Through the Council, General Manager's Office, and departments spanning health, education, natural resources, public safety, and economic development, we serve our community with sovereignty and purpose.

Our History

Treaty of Point Elliott — 1855

In 1855, the predecessor tribes of the Lummi Nation were signatory to the Treaty of Point Elliott, signed at modern-day Mukilteo. Chowitsoot, one of the Lummi leaders, signed the treaty for the Lummi "and other tribes." The treaty established the 12,562-acre Lummi Reservation near the mouth of the Nooksack River on the Lummi Peninsula.

The Lummi Nation was part of the broader fight for fishing and treaty rights, which came to a head in 1974 with United States v. Washington — commonly known as the Boldt Decision — affirming tribal treaty fishing rights.

In 1970, the Lummi Nation adopted a new constitution granting the Business Council broader powers. Today, the Lummi Nation is a nationally recognized leader in tribal self-governance.
